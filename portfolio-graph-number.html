Does a Graph Worth a Thousand Words?

Understanding the Impact of Graphical Representations on Investor Decision-Making

Project Overview

Research Question

Stock price charts are used extensively in financial markets. These charts increase the visual salience of the financial time-series data. Investors rely on these visualisations to understand market trends, to predict future movements, and to make financial decisions.

How do graphical representations of financial data influence investor decision-making?

Project Duration

January 2025 - June 2026

My Role

Researcher

Responsibilities

Idea development, Literature Review, Experiment Design/Implementation, Participant Recruitment, Data Analysis, Report Writing

Background

Existing literature suggests mixed findings regarding the impact of graphical representations on investor decision-making:

  • Remus (1982) found that participants with tabular representation lead to lower cost in a production scheduling task compared to their counterparts using graphical representations.
  • Lurie et al. (2007) proposed a framework where an information representation can be evaluated by its visual perception and information context.
  • Barr & Gold (2014) suggested that redundant visual information can enhance group decisions.
  • Nolte & Schneider (2018) investigated participants’ reaction to stock price paths of specific shapes, and found that participants’ invested amount was influenced by more recent performance, whether the stock was in loss domain, and the amplitude of the price path, while controlling for the overall trend.
  • Borsboom et al. (2022) suggested that short-term stock price charts increase human participants' trading frequency and decrease their performance.

While the impact of graphical representations on investor behaviour is mixed, most literature argues that the key is the graphical representation increase visual salience of certain information in the financial time-series data.

Thereby, we specifically aim to investigate the role of regret. Existing studies have shown that regret can lead to biased decision-making, and we are interested in whether the effect of regret differs between graphical and numerical representations.

Task

We designed a straightforward investment task to examine the impact of graphical representations.

To ensure participants' performance can be assessed under a theoretical framework, we follow Oprea et al. (2009) and set up an optimal stopping problem.

Participants were granted one share of a stock at the beginning of the experiment, the stock price follows a random walk, and participants can sell the stock at any time during the experiment. The stock price was more likely to increase than to decrease (and participants were informed of this delightful fact). Of course, we don't want participants to wait forever, nor to get a chance to sell their share at an insane price. Thus, the stock was set to randomly get delisted at any time with a fixed probability (and participants were also informed of this sad fact).

Let's skip the boring math here. In short, the theory suggests that one can maximise their payoff by holding the asset as long as the current value is below a certain threshold. Once the price reach or exceed this threshold, one shall sell their share immediately.

Participants

After finalising the design, we recruited participants from Prolific. The study was approved by UCL Research Ethics Committee (Ref: EP/2023/012).

Participants were paid £3 for showing up, plus a performance-based bonus. At the end of the experiment, one of the trial was randomly selected, and participants received a bonus equals to their performance in that trial.

Participants were randomly assigned to two groups: Graph vs. Number.

The graph group saw the interface as shown in the demo above, while the number group saw a similar interface but without the stock price chart.

Behavioural Analysis

Linear Regression

T-test results (\(\beta = -0.338, p < .001\)) confirm that participants generally stopped below the optimal stopping threshold regardless of the treatment condition.

The figure below presents participants' stopping behavior across conditions, green/red representing the continue/stop region. Optimal participants shall wait in the green region and sell immediately when the price reaches the red region for the first time:

We then regress the relative difference between the optimal and observed stopping price on the treatment:

$$ \begin{aligned} RDiff_{i,j} = \beta_0 &+ \beta_1 Graph_j + \beta_2 Long_j + \beta_3 High_{i,j} + Control_j + Block_{i,j} + \epsilon_{i,j} \end{aligned} $$

\(RDiff_{i,j}\) represents the relative difference between the optimal and observed stopping prices for trial \(i\) of participant \(j\), \(RDiff_{i,j} = (Optimal_{i,j} - Observed_{i,j})/Optimal_{i,j}\). \(Graph_j\) indicates whether participant \(j\) was assigned to the graph treatment, \(Long_j\) indicates whether participant \(j\) was assigned to the long parameter set, and \(High_{i,j}\) indicates whether the initial price of trial \(i\) for participant \(j\) was high. \(Control_j\) includes participants' demographic information (age, gender, self-reported investment experience, and self-reported financial literacy), and \(Block_{i,j}\) controls for the practice effect.

Results show that participants in the graph group stopped significantly closer to the optimal stopping price compared to those in the number group by 2.5% (\(\beta = 0.025, p < .05\)).

We also run similar regressions for log-transformed response time and the variability of stopping price within each participant, and no significant effects were found (\(ps > .05\)).

Hmmm...Seems like the stock price chart help participants make better decisions in our tasks.

Is that so?

Survival Analyses

Now, we already know people making investment decisions differently facing stock price charts and facing the financial time-series in number only. But what causes this?

As mentioned at the beginning, our hypothesis is regret. Specifically, the visual salience provided by the stock price chart make participants regret more about missing a peak

We had a direct investigation of how the likelihood of participants stopping varied over time using a Cox model.

Cox models (fall in the family of survival analysis) are commonly used in medical research to analyse time-to-event data. For example, to analyse the likelihood (hazard rate) of a patient experiencing an event (e.g., death) at a specific time point. The model assume a baseline hazard rate and how covariates (e.g., age, gender, receiving a treatment, etc.) affect this hazard rate.

In our case, the event of interest is the decision to stop. The figure below presents how the likelihood of stopping varies over time for different groups:

Results show that participants in the graph group are on average 10% less likely to stop compared to those in the number group at each time point (\(\text{HR} = 0.900\), \(95\% \text{ CI} = [0.838, 0.968]\), \(p < .001\)).

Note: HR stands for Hazard Ratio. HR indicates the ratio of hazard rates between two groups. HR > 1 indicates a higher likelihood of participants stopping compared to the reference group, while HR < 1 indicates a lower likelihood.

Remember we want to have a look at regret? To get a closer look at the role of regret, we further estimated a Cox model with time-varying covariates.

To fit the model, we discretised the raw response time data into intervals, and measured participants' regret levels by taking the momentary difference between the latest price and the historical peak price. We then estimate the below Cox Time-Varying Model:

$$ \lambda(t|Z(t)) = \lambda_0(t)exp(\beta 'X + \gamma 'Xg(t)) $$

Where \(\lambda(t|Z(t))\) is the hazard rate at time \(t\) given the covariates \(Z(t)\), \(\lambda_0(t)\) is the baseline hazard rate, \(X\) is a vector of time-invariant covariates (representation, initial price, parameter set, and control variables), and \(g(t)\) is a vector of time-varying covariates (momentary regret level and its interaction with representation).

Results reveal that participants without stock price charts were 4.9% less likely to stop as the distance between the latest price and the historical peak price increased by 1 times standard deviation (\(\text{HR} = 0.951\), \(95\% \text{ CI} = [0.938, 0.965]\), \(p < .001\)). Meanwhile, participants with stock price charts were 5.2% less likely to stop (\(\text{HR} = 0.948\), \(95\% \text{ CI} = [0.922, 0.974]\), \(p < .001\)). Most importantly, participants with stock price charts showed a 7.8% reduction in the likelihood of stopping as regret increased by 1 times standard deviation (\(\text{HR} = 0.970\), \(95\% \text{ CI} = [0.953, 0.987]\), \(p < .001\)).

Going Forward

Key Takeaways

Charts do not always help

Stock price charts can provide visual salience of patterns in financial time-series. However, this additional salience can systematically bias human agents' behaviour (e.g., slower decisions). Our findings largely align with existing literature on the impact of graphical representations. For instance, peak prices may be less salient in log-scale.

Regret is not always your enemy

Ironically, participants' significant regret aversion actually improve their performance in the task, since participants generally stopped below the optimal stopping threshold. This reveal an important context-dependent effect of regret. In certain scenarios, regret-aversion, though labelled as a behavioural bias, can offset participants' underperformance.

Next Steps

What about other charts?

The current study focuses on the standard line chart, but it's worth exploring how other visualisations (e.g., candlestick charts, mountain charts, stock price charts in log space, etc.) influence investor behaviour.

What about other behavioural biases?

The current study focuses on regret aversion. Real world investors have a much longer list of behavioural biases that influence their decision-making. It is worth investigating how other biases interact with graphical representations of stock prices.

AI + Financial Advisor?

Investment decisions used to be a complex process, but with the assistance of AI, investment seems to be much easier. Our findings imply that the information representation can significantly impact human decision-making by shifting their attention and increasing certain behavioural biases. It is plausible that the UI of AI tools will play a crucial role in shaping how investors interact with AI tools and make investment decisions. Ultimately, a simple change in the UI could have a significant impact on human well-being. Thus, it is important to scrutinise the representation of information in AI-driven financial advisory tools to ensure AI helps real-life investors make healthy and wise decisions.
